The relationship between gold and the U.S. dollar
Terry Rosser • September 20, 2025
Share this :




The price of gold and the strength of the U.S. dollar often seem to move in opposite directions, but the reality is a
bit more nuanced. Gold is priced in dollars, so the dollar’s mood and the broader economics behind it help shape
how gold trades, sometimes in simple ways and other times in surprising ones. Here’s a quick, readable guide to
what’s going on.
How the two relate (in plain terms) Dollar strength vs. gold price: When the dollar strengthens, gold often falls in dollar terms. A stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for buyers using other currencies, which can reduce demand and push prices down. Dollar weakness and inflation fears: If the dollar weakens or inflation expectations rise, gold often benefits as a hedge or store of value. In times of uncertainty or currency debasement concerns, investors flock to gold as a kind of safe haven.
The “opportunity cost” factor: Gold doesn’t pay interest or a dividend. When U.S. real yields (inflation-adjusted yields on Treasuries) rise, holding gold becomes less attractive relative to interest-bearing assets, which can push
gold prices lower. When real yields fall or become negative, gold can look comparatively more appealing.
Why the relationship isn’t perfectly stable Short-term divergences: In the short run, gold can rise even when the dollar strengthens, or fall when the dollar
weakens, due to risk sentiment, liquidity, or shifting trade flows.
Other forces at work: Central bank buying, geopolitics, supply and demand for physical gold, ETF flows, jewelry
demand, and technological or industrial uses all influence gold independently of the dollar.
Global vs. local matters: For non-U.S. investors, currency moves can amplify or mute gold’s price moves in their
local currencies, so the relationship can look diﬀerent outside the United States.
What to watch if you care about this relationship The U.S. dollar index (DXY): A broad gauge of the dollar’s strength against major currencies. A rising DXY often
correlates with weaker gold prices in USD terms, all else equal.
Real yields: Break-even inflation rates and real yields on U.S. Treasuries. Higher real yields tend to pressure gold;
lower real yields tend to support it.
Inflation expectations: If markets expect higher inflation, gold can benefit as a hedge, even if the dollar is strong.
Safe-haven demand and risk sentiment: In times of great geopolitical or financial stress, gold can rally regardless
of dollar moves.
Gold-specific factors: ETF inflows/outflows, central bank gold purchases, and physical demand (jewelry,
technology) can drive gold in directions not explained by the dollar alone.
Bottom line Gold and the U.S. dollar form a dynamic, sometimes inverse, relationship that reflects broader macro forces:
inflation, real interest rates, currency strength, and risk appetite. Understanding the dollar’s direction helps explain
much of gold’s moves, but it’s not the whole story. For a balanced view, watch the dollar index, real yields, and
inflation expectations, while also paying attention to global risk signals and central bank activity.
